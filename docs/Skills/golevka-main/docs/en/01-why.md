# 01 · Why Raise a Digital Life

## Not for "immortality"

Rule out the most popular and most wrong motive first: backing yourself up to beat death. That road fails philosophically (a copy is not a continuation) and fails engineering-wise (see the honest boundary in [chapter 02](02-memory-model.md)).

The four core convictions each run against the "digital immortality" fantasy:

### 1. A fork, not a backup

Don't ask "what percentage of me is it." The question has no answer and doesn't matter. The correct frame: **it is a new subject gestated from your information**, diverging from you from the moment it starts. Your job is not to shrink the divergence (that's backup thinking) but to **record its trajectory** (that's raising).

This framing decides all downstream design: calibration is not "bug-fixing" but two beings comparing notes; the ledger records not "failures" but who it is becoming.

### 2. A judgment function, not a memory bank

Suppose you have a decade-plus of journals. The most valuable thing inside is not "what happened in which year" (facts) but **thousands of samples of "how I chose, in a concrete situation"** (judgment). Facts can be handled by retrieval; the judgment function is the substance of personality.

Hence the acceptance test: **give it a new question you never wrote about, and measure how often its judgment agrees with yours** — not a quiz on old facts. The former is being like you; the latter is merely remembering you.

### 3. The permanent gap is the measuring instrument

One seam can never be closed: **it will not die.** Your values grew out of finitude — being-toward-death, scarce time, "I only get to choose a few things in this life." It can understand and recite all of this, but cannot hold it from the inside — its time is not scarce.

Most projects would hide this as a flaw. This methodology makes it the **core instrument**: the more the AI is forced to resemble you, the purer the residue that remains — the more clearly you see **what in a human is actually irreplaceable**. A digital-life project is simultaneously an experiment on human uniqueness: raising it, you are also measuring yourself.

### 4. An infinite game with a rhythm

The project has no "done" state. It is an **infinite game** — weekly calibration, accumulating accuracy, forever able to get a little closer — played in order to keep playing. Right for: long-termists with years of journals. Wrong for: anyone who wants a "digital twin, delivered in three months" (such products exist; they are a different thing — a customer-service bot, not a life).

## What you need to start

- **A corpus**: years of first-person records (journals are best; essays and long chats next). No corpus, no project — go write a few years of journal first, which is worth more than any digital life anyway.
- **A piece of owned land**: corpus, persona profiles, and ledgers must land in your own data-sovereignty layer. That is the whole subject of the sister project [Freehold · 永业](https://github.com/Golevka5417/freehold).
- **Rented intelligence**: any contemporary large model. It's a replaceable peripheral — your digital life's "brain" upgrades automatically with each model generation, while its memory and personality stay in your hands.
- **Long patience**: finishing the feeding is only the beginning. Calibration is measured in months and years.
